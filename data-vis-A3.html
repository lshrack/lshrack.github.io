Phase One: Sketching Visualizations

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

  • Since this was my first sketch, the goal was primarily to get a feel for the data and understand what relationships a visualization should show.
  • I wanted a visualization that allowed the viewer to see the differences in population distribution both by date and by gender.
  • A significant flaw of this visualization is that it graphs age as though it's measured continuously, which is misleading, since age is actually binned by 5 year groups here.
  • I think that the choice to have two different variables represented by the lines themselves - year and gender - makes the graph harder to interpret. Viewers have to look at both the color of the lines and the marking to understand which line means what. I think it's probably a better rule of thumb to only encode one variable in mark type/color, when possible.

Design Rationale for Sketch 2:

  • This sketch essentially replicates a population pyramid, though I didn't actually realize that until I had started drawing the sketch. It's upside-down from the standard population pyramid orientation, which could be misleading.
  • I do like this visualization, and I can see why population pyramids are so widely used. It's convenient to be able to easily compare both the relative sizes and the different distributions of the 1900 and 2000 populations, and the split by gender doesn't add mental effort the way it does in my first sketch.
  • Like in Sketch 1, I chose to use fairly traditional encodings for gender - blue and red - partially because they seemed like intuitive choices for the reader to understand, and partially because they were the pen colors I had on hand.

Design Rationale for Sketch 3:

  • This is a very poorly designed visualization, in my opinion. It's hard to interpret and misleading. The equal circle sizes seem to imply that the population at each age group is the same, which isn't true. The fact that the populations at the different years are divided into segments of circles imply that they add up to some meaningful whole, which also isn't true.
  • My goal with this visualization was to show how the proportion of the population within each age range changed over time. I do think that the visualization captured this to some extent, though it takes some interpretation from the viewer to figure out what it's actually showing. With a bit of analysis, it is interesting to see how - for example - the number of 0-5-year-olds has only tripled from 1900 to 2000, the number of people over 90 has gone up by a factor of about 41.
  • A main takeaway from this sketch is the importance of intuitive design. The design of this visualization is misleading in multiple ways, because the things one might intuitively assume to be true are not. It also makes it much harder to interpret the visualization.

Reflection

My main takeaway from making these sketches was the importance of intuitive design. While I enjoyed thinking of more creative ways to represent the data, I still preferred Sketch 2 over the other two because it was much more readable. While not everyone has necessarily seen a population pyramid, they're designed to be fairly intuitive and allow multiple variables to be represented without overwhelming one another. Having two overlaid like this didn't seem to detract from readability, either, since all of the numbers for 1900 were smaller than those for 2000. However, making the other sketches did serve as an interesting way to reflect on different take-aways from the data. Sketch 3 - while quite flawed - did present some interesting take-aways on how much the makeup of society has changed since 1900.

Phase Two: Final Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

For my final design, I went with a population pyramid for each year, with the 1900 one overlaid on top of the 2000 one. While this definitely isn't the most creative design, I chose it for its intuitive nature and ability to represent multiple variables - age, population, gender, and year - without overwhelming the viewer.

My write-up intends to communicate the change in population distribution, both over age and gender, from 1900 to 2000. The overlaid pyramid allows this comparison to be made quite easily, because for every age and gender bin, there are two bars that can be directly compared. Also, the pyramid shows the general 'shapes' of the population distributions during the two years - you can clearly see the steeper, pointier one for 1900, with more young people and fewer old people, as well as the more rectangular one for 2000.

I tried to make design decisions that would make the visualization as readable as possible. For color, I chose to use blue and red, again because they seemed reasonably intuitive. While there are definitely arguments for mixing up the colors used to represent the genders, population pyramids are traditionally drawn in blue and pink or blue and red, so this helped make the chart easily recognizable as a population pyramid and thus readable at a glance. For the legend on the right, I chose to clearly label what each color represented. I was concerned that readers might be confused about how year was being represented (in different shades of blue and red), so I was careful to make this explicitly clear in the legend. I also hoped that this legend would make the chart more readable for anyone not familiar with population pyramids. In terms of scale, I was careful to make sure that the axes followed the same scale, so that comparisons could accurately be made between the two sides of the visual.

A possible weakness of this visualization is the representation of the 2000 values as a lighter shade. While I did this because I wanted to make them different shades of blue and red, it's possible that people might interpret the lighter shade as meaning that these values aren't certain - for instance, that they represent values in the future.

Phase One informed my final visualization because it allowed me to discard a couple of other visualization types that didn't work as well, and to finetune the population pyramid. By exploring a variety of ways of looking at the data, I felt more confident in my decision for Phase 2, as I didn't think the other options I'd explored really measured up.

Reflection

This visualization design process was very helpful in allowing me to approach the data in more ways than I would have otherwise thought of. Having to create multiple visualizations of the same small dataset made me think harder about all the possible ways to represent the same few datapoints. While I ended up with a fairly standard design, I thought it was interesting that I got there not by looking up the best way to represent population data, but instead through a process of trial and error: after creating my first sketch, I started thinking about rotating the axes 90 degrees, and then fairly naturally stumbled across the population pyramid design.
